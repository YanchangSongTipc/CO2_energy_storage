Compressed carbon dioxide energy storage (CCES), as one of the compressed gas energy storage (CGES) tech-
nologies, can make the system capable of combined heat and power supply by storing and releasing electrical 
energy in the form of heat and potential energy, which is of positive significance for realizing efficient and 
comprehensive energy utilization and promoting the development of energy storage technology. A combined 
heat and power supply system based on compressed carbon dioxide energy storage (CCES-CHP) and its math -
ematical model are constructed. In order to construct a visualized operational feasibility domain, the dimen -
sionless factors γ1 and γ2 and the ratio parameters α1 and α2 are defined to reflect the coupling relationship 
between the system storage/release energy power, mass flow rate and cooling/heat release power. A dual state of 
charge (SOC) model of heat and gas storage is also constructed. Further research is conducted on the shape and 
characteristics of the operational feasibility domain under the combined heat and power mode. The change of the 
ratio of themoelectric distribution ratio dispatch and wide operating conditions are analyzed. Thus provide an 
idea for evaluating the flexibility of the system operation combined heat and power capacity and response 
tracking. Finally, the analysis process and application potential of the proposed operational feasibility domain 
analysis method were validated for a typical integrated energy system.   
1.Introduction 
With the growing consensus on achieving carbon neutrality world -
wide, the development of renewable energy sources is accelerating at an 
unprecedented pace [1]. However, the intermittency, seasonality, and 
unpredictability of these sources have highlighted the critical need for 
energy storage solutions. Physical energy storage technologies offer a 
promising approach to achieving gradient utilization of energy. This 
approach not only effectively addresses the challenge of energy con-
sumption from renewable sources but also enhances the operational 
efficiency and flexibility of energy systems. As a result, it supports the 
development of a sustainable, low-carbon, and economically viable 
energy infrastructure, which is currently the focal point of energy 
research and the driving force behind the future of energy development 
[2,3]. Among many energy storage technologies, compressed gas energy 
storage (CGES) has become a promising energy storage technology by 
virtue of its high power generation, wide range of capacity, and low 
investment cost [4]. Compressed air energy storage (CAES) is the earliest 
developed and the most mature technology among CGES. The conven -
tional non-adiabatic CAES (D-CAES) system [5] requires the stored air to 
be reheated by fuel combustion before expansion, and the energy stor-
age efficiency is not high, which has now largely fallen out of use. After 
years of evolution, a series of CAES systems without the use of fossil fuels 
have been proposed, including adiabatic CAES (A-CAES) [6], isothermal 
CAES (I-CAES) [7], and combined thermal CAES [8]. However, the most 
distinctive feature of the above types of CAES is the reliance on a large 
and high-pressure storage medium (usually underground caves, salt 
caverns, abandoned mines, etc.), which also leads to their low energy 
density. The liquid air energy storage (LAES) technology for 
low-pressure liquid storage [9], where the fact that the critical

In recent years, scholars have extensively studied the optimal design 
of the CCES system itself [12–15], which primarily provides a reference 
for studying the influence law of key CCES parameters on system per-
formance. Considering the diversification of energy demand, CCES 
technology is gradually developing towards multi-functionality. As the 
system involves the storage and release of electrical and thermal energy, 
it has the natural capability of combined heat and power supply [16]. 
Some scholars have conducted research on CCES cogeneration [17]. Liu 
et al. [18] proposed a cooling, heating and power trigeneration system 
based on transcritical Brayton cycle and carbon dioxide energy storage, 
and investigated the system through thermodynamic simulation. Zhang et al. [19] proposed a combined heat and power system based on fan 
waste heat utilization and transcritical CCES. The system cycle effi-
ciency, functional efficiency, and energy density were 0.48, 1.19, and 
11.51 kWh/m3, respectively, at a hot water distribution ratio of 0.25. He 
et al. [20] considered coupling a combined heat and power unit (CHP) 
with a CCES and analyzed the effect of the CCES system on CHP per-
formance. The results showed that the improved configuration of CCES 
with CHP achieved better performance than the basic configuration, 
with system power efficiency (SPE), power change rate (PCR), and en-
ergy storage density (ESD) increasing from 47.21 %, 12.51 %, and 0.99 
kWh/m3 to 63.07 %, 19.51 %, and 1.32 kWh/m3, respectively. How-
ever, to maintain efficient operation, the cycle pressure is very high and 
the system is limited by the ambient temperature.

vessel [21]. In addition, the storage pressure is directly related to the 
pressure and temperature of the final stage turbine outlet. The outlet of

As a complex system, the operation dispatch of CCES in the inte-
grated energy system are also more critical aspects. At present, most 
scholars have conducted extensive research on the benefit evaluation 
and dynamic operation of CAES and supercritical carbon dioxide cycle 
power plants. Safaei et al. [23] conducted an economic evaluation of the 
cogeneration operation of distributed compressed air energy storage 
(D-CAES), and the results showed that D-CAES using compressed waste 
heat for heating has considerable economic. He et al. [24] investigated 
the dynamic characteristics of adiabatic CAES and found that the sliding 
pressure charging mode is 3.92 % more energy efficient than the con-
stant pressure charging mode. Changing the cooling water flow rate can 
significantly adjust the output of cold, heat, and electrical power. Li 
et al. [25] analyzed in detail the standby characteristics of AA-CAES 
power plants under different operating conditions and established an 
optimal dispatch model for power systems containing AA-CAES power 
plants considering the standby market. Duniam et al. [26] took the su-
percritical CO2 recompression Brayton cycle as an example and found 
that some over-design of the maximum allowable pressure of the unit 
can enable more efficient cycle operation of the system under a wider 
range of off-design conditions. 
The primary duty of an energy storage system with combined heat 
and power function is to meet the customer load demand, so how to 
achieve fast dynamic load response and load tracking becomes a vital 
issue so that it can dispatch resources more flexibly, reduce system 
operation costs and promote renewable energy consumption. Guo et al. 
[27] explored the response characteristics of CAES system under the key 
regulation parameter steps, established an integrated kinetic model, and 
investigated the response characteristics of the charging and discharging 
process to the step effects of the key regulation parameters. Bian et al. 
[28] simulated the partial load performance estimation of a supercritical 
CO2 Brayton cycle and gave a strategy for the off-design performance on 
its operation following the load. 
The literature review shows that the cogeneration and load dynamic 
response dispatch of CGES is the hottest research direction at present. 
Apart from CAES, there is still relatively little research on power simu-
lation and operation dispatch for CCES cogeneration system, and a 
concise and effective analysis method has not been developed yet. Only 
Zhang et al. [29] conducted a preliminary study on the dynamic char-
acteristics of a CCES system. However, they only compared the storage 
reservoir under constant and variable pressure modes without consid -
ering the combined effects of other parameters in the system. It should 
be said that there is still a lot of work to be done in the dispatch control 
of the CCES system. 
In this paper, a compressed carbon dioxide energy storage combined 
heat and power (CCES-CHP) system operating in the medium pressure 
range is proposed, and an innovative method for constructing and 
applying an operational feasibility domain is presented. The main 
innovative ideas of this paper are elucidated as.  
● The basic construction method of the operational feasibility domain 
is constructed by coupling the electric power flow, heat transfer heat 
flow, and mass flow in the CCES-CHP system by means of con-
structing dimensionless numbers and ratio parameters;  
● A dual SOC model combining heat and gas storage for CCES-CHP is 
established, and a two-dimensional linear constraint boundary was 
constructed to reduce the complexity of image analysis.  
● For the first time, the operating range and regulation space of the 
system is characterized by the visualization of coordinate diagrams, which is an attractive way of dynamic characterization that has been 
little noticed in the literature.  
● The impact of changes of thermoelectric distribution ratio dispatch 
and wide operating conditions on the operational feasibility domain 
is studied by a typical system, while also verifying the flexibility and 
practicality of load response tracking methods. 
2.CCES-CHP system description and thermodynamic modeling 
2.1. System description 
The system schematic of CCES-CHP is shown in Fig. 1. The 
compression and expansion processes cannot be carried out simulta -
neously, while the heating process is relatively independent, and two 
operation modes of charging-heating and power generation-heating are 
considered in this paper. In the energy storage process, the low valley 
electricity or renewable energy abandoned electricity from external 
drives the compressors (C1, C2) to compress the low-pressure CO2 in the 
low-pressure tank (LPT) to a high temperature and high-pressure state, 
and the CO2 is cooled down by multi-stage compression and multi-stage 
cooling (IC1, IC2), and then stored in the high-pressure tank (HPT) in the 
form of room temperature liquid after being liquefied by water through 
the latent heat exchanger (LHE). The heat of compression in the energy 
storage process is absorbed by the heat transfer oil in the ambient 
temperature thermal oil vessel (ATV) and stored in the high-temperature 
thermal oil vessel (HTV). In the energy release process, liquid CO2 flows 
out from the high-pressure tank and is throttled and stabilized by a 
throttling valve (V2), then enters the evaporative heat exchanger (EHE) 
for complete vaporization, followed by a multi-stage heat recovery heat 
exchanger (HR1, HR2) to absorb the stored compressed heat and raise 
the temperature, and the supercritical CO2 drives the two-stage turbine 
(T1, T2) to generate electricity. The CO2 from the turbine outlet is cooled 
down by the after-expansion heat exchanger (AE-HE) and the cooler and 
returned to the low-pressure tank. In this system, the high-temperature 
heat exchanger (HG-HE) and the low-temperature heat exchanger (LG- 
HE) use the high-temperature and exothermic heat storage masses to 
provide heat at different temperatures to the customers, respectively. 
Due to the parameter setting of CO2 expansion in the turbine, there is 
still higher temperature grade heat available at the outlet of the final 
stage turbine. The accompanying heat in CCES-CHP is used to heat the 
circulating medium of the heat network to supply heat to households, 
commercial buildings, industrial parks, etc., from which the overall 
energy utilization efficiency of the system can be effectively improved. 
2.2. Thermodynamic modeling 
In order to analyze the system performance, a thermodynamic model 
of the system components was developed based on the mass conserva -
tion and energy conservation equations. The simulation assumptions 
[30–32] used to simplify the system model are shown below.  
(1) The charging/discharging power and heating power remain 
constant during a single dispatch period;  
(2) There is no heat exchange between the CO2 storage tank and the 
environment;  
(3) Neglecting heat and pressure energy losses in the heat exchanger, 
mixing and diversion piping, and connection pipes;  
(4) Disregard the effect of power consumed by the working fluid 
circulation pump;  
(5) In the process of the system undergoing a complete cycle, it is 
assumed that each time the heat storage system stored 
compression heat can be fully utilized by supplying heat to the 
user after satisfying the expansion process heating, that is, 
ignoring the difference between the minimum temperature of the 
heat storage medium outlet and the ambient temperature due to 
the presence of the actual minimum heat exchange temperature J. 3.Construction method of the operational feasibility domain of 
CCES-CHP system 
Combining the calculations mentioned above and analysis strategy of 
CHP units regarding the power generation and heat supply power con-
straints [34,35], we can find that the operational feasibility domain of

4.1. Characteristics of the operational feasibility domain  
(1) At constant compressor and turbine efficiencies, there is a 
deterministic relationship between the electrical power of the 
compressor/turbine at moment t, and the heat transfer power of 
the intercooler/heat return heat exchanger, so γ1 and γ2 are 
constant values. γ1 and γ2 can also be viewed as the slopes of the 
linear constraints for both the charge-heat and discharge-heat 
conditions.  
(2) In the operational feasibility domain, the x-axis and y-axis and 
the upper limit value constraints of their variables form a rect-
angular region, and together with other constraints, the opera -
tional feasibility domain has a polygonal shape. This indicates 
that the closer the operating feasible domain is to a rectangle, the 
more extensive the operating range, the more operating condition 
points, and the more flexible the operation.  
(3) In the operational feasibility domain, the constructed boundary 
expressions show the quantitative relationship between the pa-
rameters under the corresponding moment t-1 and moment t.

CHP can accurately track the power dispatch command from the 
customer side.

capacity and rated power of CCES-CHP should be increased in order to 
consume more rich power and better track the customer load, and the 
optimization effect of tightly changing the operating parameters is 
limited.

6.Conclusion 
In this study, a compressed carbon dioxide energy storage cogene -
ration (CCES-CHP) system is established. In order to thoroughly inves -
tigate the influence of the cogeneration characteristics of CCES-CHP and 
the customer-side flexibility dispatchable on the optimal operating 
condition region of the system, a method of determining the operational 
feasibility domain of CCES-CHP is proposed. Based on this, the shape, 
characteristics, influencing factors, and application methods of the 
operational feasibility domain are analyzed. The results can be sum-
marized as follows.  
(1) The dimensionless factor γ1, γ2 and ratio parameters α1, α2 
quantifies the complex coupling and mutual constraints between 
heat, electricity, and mass flow within the system. Among them, 
γ1 and γ2 are the slope of the linear constraint for both the charge- 
heat and discharge-heat conditions. The feasible region formed 
by the upper limit constraints of the x-axis and y-axis and their 
variables is in a two-dimensional polygonal shape.  
(2) The operational feasibility domain of CCES-CHP is time-varying, 
and the closer it is to a rectangle, the larger its operation range, 
the more operating condition points, and the more flexible its 
operation. When considering changes of dispatch command and 
wide operating conditions, the parameter correction goal is to 
make the new operating condition point tend towards the 
boundary of the operational feasibility domain. Further devel -
opment has been made of computing strategies for operational 
feasibility domain. 
(3)Finally, an integrated energy system case with CCES-CHP is in-
tegrated to analyze the influence law of typical daily thermo -
electric power distribution ratio changes and operational 
feasibility domain considering broad working conditions. It is 
found that a moderate increase in the electric load dispatch can 
improve the CCES-CHP discharge time and fully improve its 
operating economy, which verifies the application potential of the proposed operational feasibility domain analysis method. It is 
found that under the initial parameters, the ηelectricity and ηheating, 
total of CCES-CHP reach 66.7 % and 21.4 %, respectively, and the 
comprehensive energy utilization efficiency of the system can 
reach 88.1 %. 
In conclusion, the operational feasibility domain analysis method 
proposed in this paper can give full play to the advantages of CCES-CHP 
combined heat and power supply, which is of guiding significance for 
the structural improvement and operational optimization of CCES. It can 
provide some reference for capacity planning, real-time dispatch, and 
scheduling visualization of integrated energy systems.
